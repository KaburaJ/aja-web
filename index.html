Why?

Computer vision allows for non-intrusive monitoring of animal behavior without the disruptions and potential harm caused by traditional methods like GPS trackers.

Consequently, Animal pose estimation focuses on joint localisation requiring a robust model architecture due to the diverse and intricate skeletal structures of animals.

Methodology

VHR-BirdPose model, initially used for birds, was trained on the Animal Kingdom dataset, which includes 33,000 frames of over 850 species. VHR uses HRNet and attention branch to process input images for spatial and contextual feature extraction.

Model performance was evaluated using Percentage of Correct Keypoints (PCK)@0.05.

The loss function used was Mean Squared Error (MSE) between predicted and ground truth heatmaps.

Results

Comparing our model performance trained on all animals and evaluating the performance of birds against when trained on birds as of VHR-BirdPose alone, we see that our model performs better.

This indicates that the model generalises better given that birds in the dataset share similar attributes with other animals, as they are all vertebrates.

Discussion and Conclusion

The framework simplifies distribution and installation, fostering community contributions and continuous improvement.

The model struggled with frames containing multiple animals, increasing the likelihood of errors in joint fitting.

A top down approach was shown to be useful for estimating pose in uncontrolled, cluttered images, as it fixes the scale and reduces the (x, y) search region to an enlarged area around the detection window.

Future Work

We propose using object detection to dynamically localise regions of interest, reducing noise and improving robustness for better general performance.
